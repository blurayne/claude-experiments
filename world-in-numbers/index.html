How to read this

Each card shows a rough total today, a progress bar that fills as time runs from 2026 toward the year you pick on the slider, and the projected total for that year. Every category follows a study-based trajectory: humans grow, peak near 2084, then decline; livestock keeps rising with meat demand; while trees, insects, wild birds and ocean fish keep shrinking — so the projected number can go up or down as you move the slider. Two live flows show born / added and killed / lost since you opened the page; the grey line is the assumed rate per minute. Everything refreshes every 5 seconds. The est. tag marks genuinely uncertain figures — treat them as orders of magnitude.

Sources & assumptions

  • Humans — ~8.30 billion now; ~252 births / ~121 deaths per minute. Projection peaks at ~10.3 billion in 2084, then declines to ~10.2 billion by 2100. Worldometer, UN World Population Prospects 2024.
  • Trees — ~3.04 trillion; ~15 billion cut and ~5 billion planted/grown per year (net −10 billion/yr). Projection: a continued net loss to ~2.3 trillion by 2100. Crowther et al., Nature (2015).
  • Insects — ~10 quintillion (10¹⁹); born/killed are rough turnover estimates. Projection: ~1%/yr decline (to ~4.8 quintillion by 2100), reflecting the "insect decline" literature. Smithsonian.
  • Birds — ~50 billion wild birds; born/killed are a rough turnover estimate (~30%/yr mortality). Projection: ~0.6%/yr decline (to ~32 billion by 2100). Callaghan et al., PNAS (2021).
  • Fish — ~3.5 trillion ocean fish; "killed" ≈ ~1.6 trillion caught by humans per year; "born" is a rough estimate. Projection: ~0.8%/yr decline under overfishing (to ~1.9 trillion by 2100). fishcount.org.uk, Our World in Data.
  • Animals (land animals raised for food) — ~33 billion alive; ~92 billion slaughtered per year. Projection: rising with global meat demand (to ~58 billion alive by 2100). Our World in Data, Humane World, FAO projections.

Totals are anchored to mid‑2026. Where good study projections exist (humans) the curve follows them; otherwise the trend is extrapolated linearly from current net rates. This is an illustrative experiment, not a scientific instrument.
